Hizbollah 'is plotting to assassinate' Abbas
By Inigo Gilmore in Jerusalem
(Filed: 13/02/2005)

Palestinian security officials have received threats that the Palestinian president, Mahmoud Abbas, could be killed by Iranian-backed Hizbollah terrorists if he continues moves towards reconciliation with Israel.



Mr Abbas agreed a ceasefire with Israel and shook hands with its prime minister, Ariel Sharon, at last week's summit at Sharm el-Sheikh, Egypt.

Palestinian officials sounded the alarm after hearing that Hizbollah is urging militants from the Islamic group Hamas in the Gaza Strip and the West Bank to continue to attack Israel.

The threats to Mr Abbas came indirectly, via Palestinian Hizbollah contacts, but were being taken "very, very seriously", one official said.

"Many groups both here and outside do not want a ceasefire because a ceasefire means they will be without power," he said.

"As in Iraq, a lot of different powers and groups in the region are involved here and are trying to manage the resistance in our territory. Abbas is challenging their power, and so it is a dangerous path for him."

Hizbollah emerged as a dangerous force in the 1980s while fighting Israel in southern Lebanon and has since evolved into a chief financier and supporter of terrorist action in the Israeli-Palestinian conflict. The militia, which is also backed by Syria as a proxy force in its struggle with Israel over the Golan Heights, has apparently increased its influence as other sources of funding for militant groups have dried up.

Mr Abbas acknowledged that Hizbollah might threaten his life during a private meeting with two US senators - Joe Biden, a Democrat, and John Sununu, a Republican - just before last month's Palestinian election, according to an unnamed congressional official quoted in Newsweek magazine.

"He said the main threat might be less Hamas and more external," the official was quoted as saying.

Palestinian officials claim to have intercepted e-mail and other correspondence showing the bank transactions through which Hizbollah has provided funds for Palestinian terrorists.

Documents intercepted by Palestinian intelligence, and seen by The Telegraph, showed that Hizbollah cells have brought Palestinian students studying at Middle Eastern universities to Lebanon for terrorist training.

Reports last week suggested that Hizbollah operatives in Lebanon have been offering up to $100,000 (£54,000) to any Palestinian willing to carry out a suicide attack against Israel.

Ghassan Khatieb, the Palestinian labour minister, said some officials might be playing up the Hizbollah threat, perhaps to provide an external scapegoat if Mr Abbas's security clampdown fails, but he admitted that there were growing concerns about the Palestinian president's safety and welfare.

"These are very tense times and I believe he should be very careful," he said.

"But I think Mr Abbas's approach is the right one - to try to create dialogue with the militant groups while building up the security forces. Of course some extremist elements do not like what he is doing but he is determined."

Mr Abbas has promised the US and Israel that he will streamline the Palestinian security agencies in an attempt to prevent terrorist attacks.

To demonstrate his determination to stick to his commitment, he reacted swiftly at the end of last week after Palestinian militants fired more than 40 mortar shells at a Jewish settlement in the Gaza Strip.

He dismissed 20 senior security officers, including the head of public security in the occupied territories, Abdel Razek al-Majaydeh. He was also "studying a series of measures and decisions" to reinforce law and order, an official said. Many more officials appointed in the Yasser Arafat era who are viewed as corrupt and incompetent face dismissal.

Mr Abbas travelled to the Gaza Strip this weekend for talks with Hamas, the terrorist organisation with the closest links to the Palestinian leadership.

He has urged its leaders to abide by the temporary truce it agreed last month and to commit itself to a permanent ceasefire, following Israeli and Palestinian declarations of a cessation of hostilities.

Israel and the US have insisted that if further peace talks are to be held it is essential that Mr Abbas succeeds in turning his fractured and corrupt security organisations into a force that can take on militant groups.
