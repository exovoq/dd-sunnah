JERUSALEM, Dec 23 (AFP) - Bank Hapoalim of Israel and Iran's Melli Bank have been transacting business for five years in spite of a freeze in the two countries' diplomatic relations, Israeli public radio said Tuesday. Representatives from Bank Hapoalim and Melli Bank signed an agreement in Cairo in 1992 allowing the transfer of letters of credit without going through a third bank, the radio station said, citing Israeli businessmen. Such an agreement would imply clandestine commercial transactions between the two countries, which have not officially had contact since Iran's 1979 Islamic revolution. At the time, the Israeli government owned shares in Bank Hapoalim, while the Iranian government owned up to 50 percent of Melli Bank. The radio said that the two banks carried out numerous transactions between 1993 and 1994. However, a Bank Hapoalim spokesman denied that they had been doing business, saying all contact with Iranian banks ceased after the revolution, the radio reported. The Jerusalem Post reported 10 days ago that Iran had been buying Israeli high tech and medical products via Europe and that the purchases exceeded 100 million dollars a year. Under Iran's royal family, which founded Melli Bank, the country had excellent relations with Israel. After the revolution, Tehran cut ties with the Jewish state, and each country now considers the other to be its main enemy in the region.
