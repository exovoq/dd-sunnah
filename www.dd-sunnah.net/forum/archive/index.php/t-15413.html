Iran rally turns against clergy

TEHRAN, Iran, Dec. 7 (UPI) -- A supposedly anti-American rally turned into a protest against the Iranian clergy in Tehran Saturday with some 3,000 students demanding the release of a reformist leader.
Hashem Aghajari, a former Islamic revolutionary and now a college professor, was arrested Nov. 6 and sentenced to death by Iran's religious courts for demanding religious reforms.
Aghajari says that Islam needs to be freed from the clutches of the clergy. He was arrested soon after giving a speech on the need for "Islamic Protestantism" to rejuvenate the religion.
Iran's Islamic Republic News Agency reported Saturday that the students also called for the resignation of the pro-clergy judges who, they said, were trying to crush the reformist movement.
IRNA reported that the students had originally gathered to mark the day, which has been observed in Iran since 1953 when several students were killed while protesting a decision by the ex-shah to confer an honorary doctorate to Richard Nixon, then vice-president in the Eisenhower ********************istration.
Students left their classes Saturday, carrying pictures of those killed in 1953, but soon their slogans turned against the Iranian clergy, particularly the religious courts for sentencing Aghajari to death.
They demanded the resignation of all religious judges and the immediate release of Aghajari and other prisoners detained on political charges.
This led to a scuffle with the police who prevented the students from leaving the campus.
The clergy has enjoyed an increasingly firm hold over Iran since 1979, when Ayatollah Khomeini toppled the late shah and brought an Islamic government.
Since 1997, however, Iran has had reformist President Mohamed Khatami, who urges the clergy to loosen its grip on power. "Whenever in history a religion has faced freedom, it has been the religion which has sustained damage," Khatami has argued.
His moves are strongly resisted by the clergy.
However, the conflict has encouraged reformists like Aghajari to urge ordinary Muslims to protest the clergy's control.
